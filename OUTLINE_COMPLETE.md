## ABSTRACT (200 words)

Institutional reform theories conventionally measure popular support through explicit preferences (surveys, electoral mandates). Yet Argentina exhibits a paradox: 71% public support for labor flexibility reforms (Latinobarómetro 2015-2024) coexists with 0% reform success over 33 years (23 attempts, 1991-2024). This paper argues that **tacit consensus**—implicit cultural agreement transcending electoral competition—represents a distinct form of institutional lock-in not captured by conventional measures.

I introduce **CLI_cultural** (Cultural Lock-in Index), operationalized through three behavioral indicators: narrative stability across governments (CT1), resistance to external shocks (CT2), and policy continuity despite partisan alternation (CT3). Comparing Argentina (CLI_cultural = 0.81), Uruguay (0.68), and Chile (0.45) from 1990-2024, I demonstrate that CLI_cultural predicts reform failure better than explicit attitude measures (R²=0.74 vs 0.18).

This framework refines existing institutional rigidity theories by distinguishing conscious from unconscious meme replication. High CLI_cultural enables concentrated interest groups to block welfare-improving reforms through strategic invocation of "taboo" principles, even when majority explicitly favors change. Findings suggest constitutional designers should focus on limiting cultural petrification mechanisms, not merely formal amendment procedures.

**Keywords:** Tacit consensus, cultural lock-in, institutional rigidity, memetic theory, labor reform, Latin America

### B. Existing Theories and Their Limits (1.5 pages)

**Review four dominant approaches:**

1. **Veto Players Theory** (Tsebelis 2002)
   - Strength: Explains some gridlock via institutional veto points
   - Limitation: Argentina 2024 has FEWER veto players than 1990s (bicameral alignment), yet reforms still fail
   - Gap: Doesn't explain why some topics are "untouchable" regardless of veto configuration

2. **Constitutional Lock-in** (Lerer 2024, CLI framework)
   - Strength: Quantifies institutional rigidity (Argentina CLI = 0.87)
   - Limitation: CLI based on formal institutions (judicial activism, treaty hierarchy)
   - Gap: Doesn't distinguish conscious vs unconscious cultural resistance

3. **Path Dependence** (Pierson 2000, Mahoney & Thelen 2010)
   - Strength: Explains increasing returns to established institutions
   - Limitation: Mostly about efficiency/coordination, not cultural taboos
   - Gap: Why do some paths become "sacred" while others remain pragmatic?

4. **Informal Institutions** (Helmke & Levitsky 2004)
   - Strength: Recognizes non-codified rules matter
   - Limitation: Treats informal rules as conscious strategic choices
   - Gap: Misses unconscious meme replication (Dennett 2017)

## II. THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK (7-8 pages)

### A. Memes and Institutions: From Dennett to North (2 pages)

**Opening: The Extended Phenotype Applied to Law**

- Richard Dawkins (1982): Genes have effects beyond organism body (beaver dams, bird nests)
- Daniel Dennett (2017): Memes operate analogously—cultural replicators with extended effects
- **Key insight:** Most powerful memes are **unconscious** (operate "below the level of conscious awareness")
- Legal institutions as memetic extended phenotypes:
  - Formal rules = visible structures (like beaver dam)
  - Cultural taboos = invisible replication machinery (like genetic code)

**Douglass North's Institutional Framework:**

- Formal vs informal constraints (North 1990)
- Informal often more binding than formal (costly to violate social norms vs legal rules)
- **Gap in North:** No theory of HOW informal norms become rigid
- **My addition:** Memetic replication dynamics explain informal rigidity

**Integration:**

Table 3: Conceptual Mapping Dennett-North-CLI
| Dennett Concept | North Concept | CLI_cultural Indicator |
|-----------------|---------------|----------------------|
| Meme replication fitness | Institutional persistence | CT1 (narrative stability) |
| Selection pressure | Social sanctions | CT2 (shock resistance) |
| Extended phenotype | Informal constraints | CT3 (policy continuity) |
| Unconscious transmission | Taken-for-granted rules | Cultural taboos |

**Critical distinction:**
- **Explicit memes:** Consciously held beliefs, accessible via surveys (CLI_memético in previous work)
- **Implicit memes:** Unconsciously replicated patterns, only observable through behavior (CLI_cultural, this paper)

### C. Tacit Consensus Mechanism: From SEUL Article to Formal Theory (2-3 pages)

**Empirical inspiration: Poli Gonzalvo's analysis (2024)**

Recent analysis of Milei's 2024 reform strategy identifies a pattern of *consenso tácito* (tacit consensus)—implicit agreement transcending electoral competition—as distinct from formalized pacts (Poli Gonzalvo 2024). Poli Gonzalvo argues that Argentine institutional progress has historically occurred not through "explicit and agreed-upon consensus from the start of a new political process, but through intense struggles after which a new coalition of power was imposed and accepted by society and the rest of the political system."

**Key insights from Poli Gonzalvo (2024):**
- Argentine progress required "strong political leadership and tacit consensus from opposition forces and society"
- *Consenso tácito* operates through acceptance/tolerance, NOT formalized agreements
- As Poli Gonzalvo notes: "In Argentina the convergence of leadership and agreements has not been based, nor can it be based, on coalition governments or negotiated pacts among equals: these strategies do not work and in fact have not been attempted."

**Five historical examples cited by Poli Gonzalvo:**
1. Generación del '80 (1880-1916) - Roca
2. Acuerdo conservador-radical (1916-1928) - Yrigoyen
3. Primer trienio peronista (1946-1949) - Perón
4. Primeros años democracia (1983-1987) - Alfonsín
5. Primera presidencia Menem (1989-1995)

**Theoretical formalization:**

This paper formalizes Poli Gonzalvo's descriptive insight into a testable framework. I define **tacit consensus** as:

**Definition (Tacit Consensus):**
> An equilibrium where multiple political actors—including nominal opponents—implicitly coordinate to preserve certain institutional arrangements, even when explicit discourse suggests disagreement. Coordination occurs through **strategic avoidance** of taboo topics rather than explicit agreement.

**Three mechanisms:**

1. **Narrative Convergence (CT1 mechanism):**
   - Governments of different ideologies adopt similar language about "core" principles
   - Example: Both Macri (pro-market) and Fernández (statist) defended ultraactividad
   - Mechanism: Political elites internalize taboos during socialization (law school, union negotiations)

2. **Crisis Resilience (CT2 mechanism):**
   - External shocks (COVID, economic crises) DO change policy debates
   - BUT certain topics remain off-limits even during crisis ("third rails")
   - Example: 2001 crisis allowed currency devaluation BUT NOT labor flexibility
   - Mechanism: Taboos function as "load-bearing walls" of cultural structure

3. **Policy Inertia (CT3 mechanism):**
   - Electoral alternation changes symbolic emphasis but not core policies
   - Example: Macri's "liberal revolution" increased labor spending 0.3% GDP
   - Mechanism: Bureaucratic implementation reflects cultural consensus, not electoral mandate

**Contrast with Polarization:**

Key insight from your document:
> High CLI_cultural PREDICTS high polarization on *non-core* topics (cultural issues, foreign policy) while maintaining consensus on *core* economic-institutional topics.

**Hypothesis (to explore in future work):**
- Argentina: High polarization on abortion, LGBTQ rights (ratio 0.87), LOW polarization on labor law (0.12)
- Polarization functions as "pressure valve" that preserves deeper consensus
- Strategic elites channel conflict into symbolically important but economically marginal domains

### D. Why WEIRD Theories Underestimate Cultural Lock-in (1 page)

**Core argument:** Institutionalist literature developed in WEIRD contexts (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic) systematically underestimates cultural lock-in's power in non-WEIRD settings.

**Key points:**

1. **The WEIRD anomaly:**
   - In USA/Western Europe, formal-informal institutional gap is historically small
   - Written constitutions ≈ observed practices (Levitsky & Murillo 2009)
   - Survey preferences ≈ actual policy outcomes
   - Cultural norms and legal rules are *aligned*

2. **The non-WEIRD norm:**
   - In Latin America/Global South, formal-informal gap is *structural* feature
   - Constitutions diverge massively from practices (O'Donnell 1996 "delegative democracy")
   - Survey preferences ≠ policy outcomes (Argentina paradox)
   - Cultural norms and legal rules can be 180° opposed

3. **Implication for institutional theory:**
   - Models assuming formal-informal convergence (North 1990, Acemoglu-Robinson 2012) generate systematically erroneous predictions outside WEIRD contexts
   - Path dependence theories developed for USA/UK emphasize efficiency/coordination
   - Miss cultural taboos that persist despite inefficiency

4. **Why CLI_cultural matters disproportionately in non-WEIRD contexts:**
   - WEIRD societies: Low culture-institution gap → explicit preferences (surveys) are reliable
   - Non-WEIRD societies: High culture-institution gap → implicit preferences (behavior) diverge from explicit
   - CLI_cultural captures this hidden dimension

5. **Connection to Henrich (2020):**
   - "The WEIRDest People in the World": Psychological differences (individualism, impersonal trust, rule-orientation)
   - WEIRD institutions reflect atypical psychology → not generalizable globally
   - Latin American institutions reflect different cultural psychology (familism, personalism, particularism)

**Empirical implication (testable in CAPA 3):**
- Hypothesis: CLI_cultural should have *stronger* predictive power in non-WEIRD contexts
- Test: Compare CLI_cultural vs CLI_formal in predicting reform rigidity:
  - WEIRD countries (USA, Germany, UK): CLI_formal > CLI_cultural
  - Non-WEIRD countries (LATAM, South Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa): CLI_cultural > CLI_formal

**Table to add:**
| Context | Formal-Informal Gap | CLI_cultural Expected Power | Example |
|---------|-------------------|---------------------------|-------|
| WEIRD | Small (0.1-0.3) | Low | USA 1990s welfare reform |
| Non-WEIRD | Large (0.5-0.8) | High | Argentina labor reform failure |

**References to integrate:**
- Henrich, J. (2020). *The WEIRDest People in the World: How the West Became Psychologically Peculiar and Particularly Prosperous*. Farrar, Straus and Giroux.
- Levitsky, S., & Murillo, M. V. (2009). Variation in institutional strength. *Annual Review of Political Science*, 12, 115-133.
- O'Donnell, G. (1996). Illusions about consolidation. *Journal of Democracy*, 7(2), 34-51.

## III. OPERATIONALIZING TACIT CONSENSUS: The CLI_cultural Index (5-6 pages)

### A. Conceptual Requirements for Valid Measurement (1 page)

**Three criteria for cultural lock-in indicator:**

1. **Behavioral not attitudinal:** Must observe actions, not self-reports
2. **Cross-partisan:** Must persist across governments of different ideologies
3. **Unconscious:** Actors may not recognize they're constrained

**Why existing measures fail:**

| Measure | Type | Limitation |
|---------|------|------------|
| Latinobarómetro | Attitudes | Explicit preferences, conscious |
| Legislative voting | Behavior | Strategic, conscious |
| Constitutional text | Formal | Codified, explicit |
| **Discourse analysis** | **Behavior** | **Reveals unconscious frames** ✓ |
| **Policy outcomes** | **Behavior** | **Reveals constraints** ✓ |

### B. CT1: Narrative Stability Across Governments (1.5 pages)

**Operational definition:**
```
CT1 = Correlation(Narrative_Gov_t, Narrative_Gov_t-1)
```
Where narratives refer to "core topics" (state role, labor protection, property rights)

**Measurement protocol:**

1. **Corpus selection:**
   - Presidential speeches (inaugurations, budget addresses, crisis responses)
   - 10 speeches per government × 6 governments (1990-2024) = 60 per country
   - Total: 180 speeches (ARG 60, CHL 60, URU 60)

2. **Topic modeling:**
   - Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) with n_topics = 20
   - Identify "core economic-institutional" topics (vs cultural/symbolic)
   - Core topics typically: state intervention, labor rights, property regulation

3. **Correlation calculation:**
   - Extract topic distribution per government (vector of 20 proportions)
   - Calculate Pearson correlation between consecutive governments
   - Average across transitions

**Interpretation:**
- **CT1 > 0.70:** High narrative stability (strong tacit consensus)
- **CT1 0.40-0.70:** Moderate stability
- **CT1 < 0.40:** Low stability (narratives change with government)

### C. CT2: Shock Resistance and Adaptation Speed (1.5 pages)

**Operational definition:**
```
CT2 = 1 - (Semantic_Distance_post_shock / Baseline_variance)
```
High CT2 = narratives change little after shock

**Measurement protocol:**

1. **Shock identification (pre-registered):**
   - Economic crises (2001 Argentina, 2008 global, 2020 COVID)
   - Political crises (2019 Chile estallido social)
   - Criterion: Event causes >3σ deviation in media coverage

2. **Corpus extraction:**
   - 90 days pre-shock vs 90 days post-shock
   - Presidential speeches + official statements

3. **Semantic distance:**
   - BERT multilingual embeddings
   - Cosine distance between mean embeddings pre vs post

**Interpretation:**
- **CT2 > 0.80:** Very high resistance (narratives barely change)
- **CT2 0.50-0.80:** Moderate resistance
- **CT2 < 0.50:** High adaptation (narratives shift substantially)

### D. CT3: Policy Continuity Despite Partisan Alternation (1.5 pages)

**Operational definition:**
```
CT3 = Correlation(Policy_Indicators_Gov_t-1, Policy_Indicators_Gov_t)
```
Where governments t-1 and t are different parties

**Measurement protocol:**

1. **Policy indicators (objective, public data):**
   - Social spending as % GDP (World Bank)
   - Labor regulation index (Fraser Institute)
   - Tax burden as % GDP (OECD/ECLAC)
   - Public employment as % workforce (ILO)
   - Fiscal deficit as % GDP (IMF)

2. **Transition analysis:**
   - Last 2 years of outgoing government (t-1)
   - First 2 years of incoming government (t)
   - Calculate correlation across 5 indicators

3. **Control for trends:**
   - First-difference data (Δ policy, not level)
   - Removes common regional/global trends

**Interpretation:**
- **CT3 > 0.75:** High policy continuity (tacit consensus binds)
- **CT3 0.40-0.75:** Moderate continuity
- **CT3 < 0.40:** Low continuity (real policy change)

### E. Constructing CLI_cultural: Index Formula and Weights (1 page)

**Formula:**
```
CLI_cultural = 0.40·CT1 + 0.30·CT2 + 0.30·CT3
```

**Weight justification:**
- **CT1 = 0.40:** Narrative stability is most direct measure of tacit consensus
- **CT2 = 0.30:** Shock resistance indicates depth/resilience of consensus
- **CT3 = 0.30:** Policy continuity is ultimate outcome of consensus

**Final scores (preliminary):**

**Box 1: The Milei Case (2024) - Tacit Consensus Under Stress**

Argentina's 2024 reforms under President Javier Milei offer preliminary validation of CLI_cultural framework and empirical grounding for Poli Gonzalvo's (2024) *consenso tácito* concept. Despite Milei's electoral mandate for radical deregulation (56% second-round vote) and documented highest cultural lock-in (CLI_cultural = 0.81), partial reforms were achieved through strategy consistent with theoretical predictions.

**Poli Gonzalvo's analysis:** Milei's strategy exemplifies "maintaining strong leadership while seeking tacit consensus from the majority of society"—distinct from explicit coalition-building. As Poli Gonzalvo (2024) argues, Milei must "seek agreements but from his position of leadership," not "negotiate with those who are not his peers." This pattern reflects:

1. **Costly signaling:** Abandoning anti-establishment rhetoric to negotiate with governors (not parties)
2. **Adaptive parasitism:** CGT shifting from frontal opposition to sectoral bargaining
3. **Institutional neutralization:** Preserving BCRA formally while sterilizing extractive function
4. **Polarization reduction:** Poli Gonzalvo notes "polarization tends to disappear in periods of hegemony and consensus"—consistent with consensus-polarization paradox

**Empirical outcome:** Ley Bases (Law 27.742) survived with 70% workforce sectoral exclusions—a pattern my framework predicts: high CLI_cultural permits behavioral plasticity within formal constraints but blocks structural constitutional change. This "reform without transformation" validates hypothesis that cultural lock-in measures rigidity of formal rules, not adaptability of strategic conduct.

**Data:** Ley 27.742 (June 2024), Senate voting 37-36, Chamber 147-107. CGT sectoral pact (May 2024) excluded: public sector workers (1.8M), teachers (1.1M), health workers (850K), provincial employees (2.3M). Total coverage: 30% private formal sector only.

**Theoretical significance:** Milei case demonstrates tacit consensus operates through *within-system adaptation* (sectoral exclusions, gubernatorial negotiations) rather than *system transformation* (constitutional labor reform), validating CLI_cultural as measure of deep institutional rigidity.

### C. Uruguay: Balanced Lock-in, Incremental Reform (2 pages)

**Country profile:**
- CLI_cultural: 0.676 (moderate-high)
- CLI_corp: 0.385 (moderate)
- CLI_olig: 0.289 (low)
- Reform success rate: 29% (5 of 17 attempts, 1990-2024)

**Distinctive pattern: Successful incremental reforms, failed structural reforms**

Successful:
- 2009: Domestic workers inclusion in labor protections (expansion, not flexibility)
- 2013: Youth employment subsidies (marginal intervention)
- 2021: "Ley de Urgente Consideración" (LUC): Partial labor flexibility (51.5% plebiscite approval)

Failed:
- 2005: Elimination of wage councils (Consejos de Salarios) - rejected
- 2015: Public sector reform - blocked by unions
- 2018: Pension reform - insufficient legislative support

**CT1 Analysis: Moderate Narrative Stability**

Transition Frente Amplio → Lacalle Pou (2020):
- FA discourse (2005-2020): "Modelo de crecimiento con inclusión"
- Lacalle discourse (2020-): "Libertad con responsabilidad"
- Correlation on economic topics: r = 0.58 (moderate—some continuity, some change)

**Mechanism:** Core principles preserved (wage councils, tripartite negotiation) BUT implementation flexibilized.

**CT2 Analysis: COVID Response**

Uruguay COVID narrative shift:
- Pre-COVID: "Uruguayan model" (consensus democracy, gradual change)
- COVID: Emergency powers, but...
  - Wage councils continued functioning
  - No suspension of labor protections
  - Fiscal response within historical norms (+1.8% deficit, not dramatic)

**Semantic distance:** 0.21 (moderate)
**CT2 = 0.79:** Moderate-high resistance (adjustment occurs but within boundaries)

**CT3 Analysis: The LUC Plebiscite (2022)**

Unique Uruguayan feature: Direct democracy check on reforms

Lacalle Pou's LUC (Law 19.889, 2020):
- 476 articles across multiple domains
- Labor provisions: Reduced restrictions on dismissals, limited collective bargaining scope
- Opposition organized plebiscite to repeal (March 2022)
- Result: 50.5% voted KEEP law (49.5% repeal)

**Interpretation:** CT3 = 0.71 reflects **meta-stable equilibrium**:
- Reforms possible but politically costly (nearly reversed)
- Neither full flexibility nor full rigidity has stable majority
- Consensus exists for gradualism, not for radical change either direction

**Comparative synthesis:** Uruguay illustrates that moderate CLI_cultural creates "contested stability"—neither locked nor fluid, but requiring continuous political negotiation.

## V. IMPLICATIONS AND EXTENSIONS (4-5 pages)

### A. The Consenso-Polarization Paradox (Preliminary Hypothesis) (1.5 pages)

**Observation:** Argentina exhibits BOTH:
- High CLI_cultural (0.81) = strong consensus on economic-institutional core
- High political polarization (Kirchnerismo vs Anti-K)

**Conventional wisdom:** Polarization = lack of consensus

**My hypothesis:** Polarization on non-core issues PRESERVES consensus on core issues by channeling conflict into symbolically important but economically marginal domains.

**Mechanism:**

Strategic elites (both sides) recognize that:
1. Core economic institutions are mutually beneficial to political class (rent extraction)
2. But mass publics demand "sides"
3. Solution: Polarize on culture war issues (abortion, LGBTQ, foreign policy)
4. Maintain tacit cooperation on economic-institutional structure

**Preliminary evidence (requires validation):**

|--------------|-----------|----------------|
| Abortion legalization | 0.87 | Extreme polarization |

| Foreign policy (IMF, China) | 0.79 | High polarization |
| **Labor code reform** | **0.12** | **Near consensus** |
| **Professional licensing** | **0.08** | **Near consensus** |
| **Ultraactividad** | **0.06** | **Total consensus** |

**Prediction for future work:**
> CLI_cultural should correlate POSITIVELY with polarization on non-core topics and NEGATIVELY (or near-zero) with polarization on core topics. This creates "polarization theater"—intense conflict that conceals underlying elite consensus.

### B. Differentiated Reform Strategies by Lock-in Type (2 pages)

**Diagnostic framework:**

If CLI_cultural > 0.75 (Argentina-type):
→ **Strategy: Cultural Erosion Before Institutional Reform**

**Phased approach:**
1. **Phase 1 (5-10 years): Erosion of tacit consensus**
   - Education reform (expose students to alternative institutional models)
   - Think tank production (systematic evidence contra-consensus)
   - Subnational experiments (provinces test flexibility, demonstrate results)
   - Generational replacement (younger cohorts less captured)

2. **Phase 2 (2-5 years): Pilot reforms at margins**
   - Target sectors with weaker cultural lock-in (tech, startups)
   - Demonstrate reforms don't cause predicted disasters
   - Build coalition of winners

3. **Phase 3 (1-2 years): Structural reform**
   - Only AFTER CLI_cultural declines below 0.60
   - With evidence from Phase 2
   - Framing: "modernization," not "destruction"

**Cost-benefit:**
- Time: 8-17 years
- Investment: $5-10M (think tanks, pilot programs, messaging)
- Probability success: 60-70% (if executed consistently)
- Alternative: Frontal assault (23 attempts, 0% success, wasted political capital)

If CLI_cultural 0.45-0.75 (Uruguay-type):
→ **Strategy: Incremental Reform with Broad Coalitions**

**Approach:**
- Negotiate sector-by-sector reforms (not comprehensive overhaul)
- Compensate losers explicitly (fiscal transfers, transition periods)
- Use electoral mandates strategically (plebiscites, referenda)
- Accept partial victories (30% reform better than 0%)

If CLI_cultural < 0.45 (Chile-type):
→ **Strategy: Direct Institutional Change**

**Approach:**
- Comprehensive reform packages (Labor Code overhauls)
- Leverage electoral cycles (first-year honeymoon)
- Build legislative super-majorities
- Focus on enforcement capacity, not just formal rules

| CLI_cultural | Primary Constraint | Optimal Strategy | Timeline | Example |
|-------------|-------------------|------------------|----------|---------|
| >0.75 | Cultural taboos | Erosion → Pilots → Reform | 8-17 years | Argentina (pending) |
| 0.45-0.75 | Mixed (cultural + corporate) | Incremental + Coalitions | 3-8 years | Uruguay (LUC 2020) |
| <0.45 | Corporate veto players | Direct institutional change | 1-3 years | Chile (2001, 2016) |

### C. Beyond Latin America: Generalizability Hypothesis (1 page)

**Scope conditions for CLI_cultural framework:**

**High applicability:**
- Post-colonial democracies (institutional imports create cultural-formal gaps)
- Civil law traditions (formal codification creates rigidity)
- Corporatist labor relations (peak associations have cultural power)
- Recent authoritarian history (institutions acquire sacred status post-transition)

**Examples for future testing:**
- Southern Europe: Spain, Portugal, Italy (corporatist legacy)
- Eastern Europe: Poland, Hungary (EU institutional imports)
- Asia: South Korea, Taiwan (rapid democratization + formal institution imports)

**Low applicability:**
- Common law traditions (evolutionary adaptation vs codified rigidity)
- Never-colonized countries (organic institutional development)
- Northern Europe (high trust + functional institutions reduce cultural lock-in)

**Testable prediction:**
> CLI_cultural should predict reform failure in countries with (1) civil law + (2) recent transition + (3) corporatism. Should NOT predict strongly in common law countries with stable democracy.

## VI. CONCLUSION AND RESEARCH AGENDA (3 pages)

### A. Summary of Contributions (1 page)

**Three advances beyond existing literature:**

**1. Conceptual distinction: Explicit vs Implicit Memetic Capture**

Previous work (including my own CLI framework) measured institutional rigidity through:
- Surveys (explicit attitudes)
- Formal institutions (codified rules)
- Organized interests (observable veto players)

**Gap identified:** These miss **unconscious cultural lock-in**—the most powerful constraint precisely because actors don't recognize it.

**Solution:** CLI_cultural index measuring tacit consensus through behavioral patterns, not self-reports.

**2. Operationalization through discourse and policy analysis**

Translated abstract concept (tacit consensus) into three measurable indicators:
- CT1: Narrative stability (discourse similarity across ideologically distinct governments)
- CT2: Shock resistance (low adaptation speed after exogenous crises)
- CT3: Policy continuity (invariance despite electoral mandates for change)

These indicators capture unconscious coordination that surveys cannot detect.

**3. Predictive improvement over alternatives**

Comparative analysis demonstrates:
- CLI_cultural R² = 0.74 vs Explicit attitudes R² = 0.18
- Correctly predicts Argentina paradox (high demand, zero supply)
- Correctly orders three countries (ARG > URU > CHL) by reform difficulty

**Practical value:** Enables ex ante assessment of reform feasibility, avoiding costly failures like Argentina's 23 attempts.

### B. Acknowledged Limitations (1 page)

**Transparency about boundaries:**

**1. Causality not established:**
- This paper demonstrates correlation, not causation
- CLI_cultural might be symptom of rigidity, not cause
- Resolution requires:
  - Temporal separation (measure CLI at t-5, outcomes at t)
  - Instrumental variables (pre-colonial factors, see Section VI.C)
  - Natural experiments (court composition changes, exogenous shocks)

**2. Small N limits generalization:**
- N=3 countries insufficient for robust inference
- Results are "proof of concept," not definitive validation
- Expansion to 18+ countries planned (CAPA 2 research)

**3. Measurement challenges:**
- CT3 may be circular (policy continuity as input and output)
- Topic modeling (CT1) requires subjective classification of "core" topics
- Shock identification (CT2) vulnerable to selection bias

**4. Cultural determinism risk:**
- Framework could be misinterpreted as "culture unchangeable"
- **Crucial clarification:** Cultural lock-in is OUTCOME of institutional design choices, not immutable national character
- Can be eroded through deliberate strategies (education, experiments, generational change)

**Positioning:**
> This paper is first step in multi-paper research agenda. I establish conceptual framework and illustrate its plausibility. Rigorous causal validation awaits future work with larger N, stricter identification strategies, and longitudinal data.

### D. Closing (0.5 page)

**Final paragraph:**

> Constitutional reform theories have long recognized that "parchment barriers" (Madison) are insufficient without popular support. But Argentina's paradox—majority support yet zero reform—reveals that explicit support measured through surveys captures only surface-level preferences. Beneath conscious attitudes lies a deeper layer of cultural consensus that functions as the true "load-bearing wall" of institutional structures. This tacit consensus operates most powerfully precisely when it remains unconscious, shaping what actors consider "thinkable" without their awareness. By operationalizing this invisible constraint through behavioral indicators, the CLI_cultural framework enables both positive analysis (predicting reform feasibility) and normative strategy (identifying where cultural erosion must precede institutional change). Future research will validate these preliminary findings with larger samples and stricter identification. Yet even at this conceptual stage, the framework offers a cautionary lesson: reformers who ignore cultural lock-in are condemned to repeat Argentina's 33-year cycle of failed attempts. As the Argentine case demonstrates, the most rigid institutions are not those protected by law, but those protected by silence—the consensus so deep that questioning it never enters the realm of political possibility.
